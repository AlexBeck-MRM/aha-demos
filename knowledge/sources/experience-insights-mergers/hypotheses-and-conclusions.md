## 1. Symptom and condition content

### Why this research existed
- AHA needs disease and condition pages to support fast understanding, trust, and clear next steps on high-traffic informational journeys.

### What the hypotheses were testing
- `high confidence` Users trust health information faster when the essential answer is contained on one clearly structured page rather than split across multiple pages or buried under weak hierarchy.
- `high confidence` Users orient faster when the page brings together the condition, symptoms, urgency, causes, and next steps in one place.
- `high confidence` Surfacing emergency symptoms and urgent action early should improve confidence and reduce dangerous hesitation.
- `high confidence` Shorter sections, plainer language, and descriptive headings should outperform dense, academic-feeling prose.
- `high confidence` Pages should feel more trustworthy when the visual tone is clinically calm and information-led rather than decorative, overly branded, or driven by generic lifestyle imagery.

### Provisional conclusions
- `high confidence` Strong support for fuller single-page guidance on symptom and condition journeys.
- `high confidence` Emergency cues and next steps should sit high on the page.
- `high confidence` Explanatory visuals help more than generic lifestyle photography on these pages.
- `high confidence` Plain-language chunking, visible hierarchy, and descriptive section labels materially improve comprehension.
- `high confidence` A restrained, clinically calm visual tone builds more trust than overly emotional or promotional styling.

### Reusable design implication
- Use this source when deciding how serious, structured, and information-led symptom or condition pages should feel.

## 2. Donation, volunteering, and staged engagement

### Why this research existed
- Client input showed that donation was being prompted too early and too often, the value of giving was not always clear, volunteering was not yet a strong path, and the current experience did not adapt well to different user states.

### What the hypotheses were testing
- `medium-high confidence` If AHA builds clearer ladders from lighter engagement into heavier commitments, users may move more naturally from updates or participation into donation.
- `medium-high confidence` Donation asks may work better in lower-pressure, impact-led situations supported by human stories, visible outcomes, and contributor recognition rather than raw donation prompts alone.
- `medium-high confidence` Donation asks tied to a clear outcome, supported by human stories and visible need, should feel more motivated and less abstract.
- `medium-high confidence` Proof and recognitional signals can help, but only when they feel modest and socially appropriate.
- `medium-high confidence` A single donation ask across information-heavy pages is unlikely to suit the full range of user states and needs.

### Provisional conclusions
- `medium-high confidence` For soft proof-based giving, visible outcomes and beneficiary stories matter more than generic donation pop-up tactics.
- `medium-high confidence` Later-stage engagement signals, clearer missions, and tangible proof of impact are stronger than pressure-heavy prompts.
- `medium-high confidence` AHA should probably reduce donation friction on high-information pages and be more selective about where donation is a primary versus secondary action.
- `medium-high confidence` There is good support for a staged engagement model: lighter actions first, then volunteering, advisory participation, value updates, and donation as context becomes more personal.

### Reusable design implication
- Use this source to argue for value-first, trust-first engagement design instead of defaulting to immediate donation pressure.

## 3. Editorial packaging and curation

### Why this research existed
- AHA publishes a wide range of editorial content intended to help people live better, not just understand disease. The research tested how trusted organisations make editorial experiences feel more engaging, current, and curated through tone, imagery, and brand behavior.

### What the hypotheses were testing
- `medium confidence` Story-led, curated editorial surfaces may feel more intentional and engaging when the editorial logic is visible across the experience.
- `medium confidence` A clear programming model may matter more than simply proving content breadth. Theme-based curation and repeated visual logic may help people browse more intentionally.
- `medium confidence` More dynamic or mood-led imagery can make editorial areas feel fresher, but only if topic clarity stays intact.
- `medium confidence` Motion, gradients, and livelier surface signals may help editorial areas feel current, but they should not replace information scent or blur the topic being addressed.

### Provisional conclusions
- `medium confidence` Story- and topic-led curation can help reposition editorial content as something people return to, not just something they pass through.
- `medium confidence` Theme-based programming appears more useful than foregrounding sheer volume or surface novelty.
- `medium confidence` Editorial energy is helpful, but it still needs a clear sense of topic, role, and navigation logic.
- `medium confidence` Dynamic imagery and motion are strongest when they amplify editorial identity without obscuring topic clarity or navigation logic.

### Reusable design implication
- Use this source when shaping Route A styleframe ideas, homepage storytelling modules, or editorial hub patterns that need to feel alive without becoming unfocused.

## 4. Navigation, search, labels, and recovery

### Why this research existed
- AHA has a broader findability problem across navigation, search, labels, breadcrumbs, and recovery paths. Users arrive with different health vocabularies and confidence levels, rely on lay terms, mistype clinical language, and struggle to recover after wrong turns.

### What the hypotheses were testing
- `high confidence` If AHA builds hubs, category pages, and index views using clear, familiar pathways, users should understand where they are more clearly without needing to backtrack.
- `high confidence` Top-level navigation should express route choices and content clusters, not carry the entire burden of findability by itself.
- `high confidence` If AHA gives users more than one way to reach the same information, users who fail in one channel should recover more easily and browse with less uncertainty.
- `high confidence` Search should map lay language to medical vocabulary and adjacent topics so people can still succeed with imprecise queries.

### Provisional conclusions
- `high confidence` Breadcrumbs and visible recovery paths are still important for AHA, but only if they connect to meaningful user routes instead of acting as shallow formal structure.
- `high confidence` If AHA simplifies top-level navigation, it should do so for the right traffic, not as a single all-purpose route. Local navigation, labels, A-Z browse, and in-page navigation should be treated as deliberate parts of the same system.
- `high confidence` AHA should simplify top-level navigation toward audience and task language, not internal structure, and reinforce this with clear labels and local wayfinding.
- `high confidence` Vernacular mapping should be treated as a priority experience area, not just an SEO or metadata exercise.

### Reusable design implication
- Use this source to validate route decisions around floating navigation, breadcrumbs, scoped search, search-result recovery, and plain-language labels.

## 5. Homepage mission clarity and audience orientation

### Why this research existed
- Users recognise the American Heart Association but do not quickly understand what it does, how its initiatives connect, or where to go next. The homepage currently does not explain the organisation's purpose clearly enough.

### What the hypotheses were testing
- `high confidence` A plain, quick mission statement near the top of the homepage should reduce first-view confusion and make AHA feel more coherent.
- `high confidence` Users should find the homepage easier to scan when it avoids competing sub-brands, audience messages, value statements, and proof points all at once.
- `high confidence` If the homepage foregrounds what AHA actually does and who it helps before promoting secondary asks, users should trust the experience more quickly.
- `high confidence` Naming the main content areas in clearer, broader language should help users understand the overall organisation model.
- `high confidence` Bringing signature brand language and visual architecture into the homepage should help the site feel more like one organisation and less like a loose set of programs.

### Provisional conclusions
- `high confidence` Evidence strongly supports a single, concrete explanation of what AHA does at the top of the homepage.
- `high confidence` AHA should reduce message competition in the hero and create a smaller number of clearer top-level choices.
- `high confidence` The homepage should likely place its strongest narrative and value cues before deeper proof signals, not bury them under secondary modules.
- `high confidence` The site should feel coherent at a system level, not just through isolated copy moments. Shared narrative, recurring page patterns, and signature brand architecture can carry that coherence.
- `high confidence` Broader top-level language and clearer brand structure outperform jargon or fragmented messaging.

### Reusable design implication
- Use this source to justify homepage simplification, clearer mission framing, stronger audience orientation, and more disciplined message hierarchy.

## Cross-cutting takeaways for ideation and validation
- Trust grows when AHA explains itself quickly, names the task clearly, and shows what to do next without ceremony.
- Navigation should be treated as a system of routes and recovery tools, not a single menu problem.
- Donation and volunteering should feel staged, contextual, and earned.
- Editorial areas can be more expressive, but not at the cost of topic clarity or navigation confidence.
- High-intent health content should stay calm, structured, and visibly useful before it tries to be emotive.
- Mission clarity and message hierarchy are not homepage polish issues; they are core comprehension issues.
