# High Blood Pressure

## Editorial intent

This page should give people useful answers quickly, then let them keep reading without forcing them into a maze of subpages. It should be more useful than a landing page, but lighter than a medical textbook.

The right balance is:

- clear first-screen orientation
- short but meaningful chapter intros
- practical action blocks
- simple contextual links to tools, specialist topics, and related pages
- no second navigation tree inside the sidebar

The page should work for someone newly diagnosed, someone checking a reading at home, someone caring for a family member, or someone trying to decide whether a reading needs urgent care.

High blood pressure is common, often silent, and manageable. It means the force of blood moving through your arteries stays too high over time. When it is not controlled, it can strain the heart, blood vessels, brain, kidneys, and eyes.

Use this guide to understand your numbers, know when a reading may be urgent, and choose practical next steps for lowering and managing blood pressure.

### Emergency cue

If your blood pressure is higher than `180/120 mm Hg`, wait at least one minute and take it again. If the second reading is still that high and you have chest pain, shortness of breath, back pain, numbness, weakness, a change in vision, difficulty speaking, or another new concerning symptom, call `911` right away.

## Overview

### Section title

High blood pressure at a glance

### Intro

Blood pressure is the force of blood pushing against the walls of your arteries. A reading can rise and fall during the day, but repeated high readings need attention. High blood pressure, also called hypertension, means those numbers stay high enough over time to increase strain on the body.

High blood pressure is not a personal failure. Many people can lower risk with regular monitoring, daily habits, medication when needed, and support from a care team.

### Key things to know

- High blood pressure often has no symptoms.
- Your numbers matter even when you feel well.
- A single high reading is not always a diagnosis, but repeated high readings should be checked.
- Small daily changes can help.
- Medication may be part of the plan, and that is common.
- Very high readings with symptoms need emergency care.

### Why it matters

Uncontrolled high blood pressure can quietly damage blood vessels over time. That can raise the risk of stroke, heart attack, heart failure, kidney disease, vision loss, and other cardiovascular problems.

## Symptoms

### Section title

High blood pressure is often silent

### Intro

Many people with high blood pressure do not feel different. That is why symptoms are not a reliable way to know whether your blood pressure is high. The only way to know is to measure it.

This section should help people separate everyday monitoring from situations that need immediate help.

### When to get urgent help

If your blood pressure is higher than `180/120 mm Hg`, check it again after at least one minute. Call `911` if it is still that high and you have:

- chest pain
- shortness of breath
- back pain
- numbness or weakness
- a change in vision
- difficulty speaking
- confusion, fainting, or another new serious symptom

If the reading is still higher than `180/120 mm Hg` but you do not have emergency symptoms, contact your health care professional right away for instructions.

### What to track

Keep a simple record of recent readings, time of day, medications, missed doses, symptoms, and questions for your care team.

## Causes

### Section title

What can raise blood pressure

### Intro

High blood pressure usually does not have one simple cause. It can be shaped by age, family history, health conditions, medicines, sleep, stress, access to care, and everyday habits.

Some risk factors can be changed. Others cannot. The page should help people focus on what they can do next without making blood pressure feel like an individual willpower problem.

### Common contributors

- family history
- getting older
- diabetes, kidney disease, sleep apnea, or other health conditions
- some medicines or supplements
- eating patterns high in sodium
- not getting enough physical activity
- drinking too much alcohol
- tobacco use
- stress and poor sleep
- weight that adds strain on the heart and blood vessels

## Diagnosis

### Section title

Understand your blood pressure numbers

### Intro

Blood pressure is measured with a cuff and recorded as two numbers. The top number measures pressure when the heart beats. The bottom number measures pressure when the heart rests between beats.

Diagnosis depends on patterns, not one isolated reading. A health care professional can confirm whether your numbers show high blood pressure and what goal is right for you.

### Blood pressure categories

| Category | Top number | Bottom number |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Normal | less than `120` | and less than `80` |
| Elevated | `120-129` | and less than `80` |
| Stage 1 hypertension | `130-139` | or `80-89` |
| Stage 2 hypertension | `140` or higher | or `90` or higher |
| Very high | higher than `180` | and/or higher than `120` |

### Home monitoring basics

Home monitoring can help you and your care team see patterns between visits.

Before a reading:

- use a validated upper-arm monitor when possible
- sit with your back supported and feet flat
- rest your arm at heart level
- avoid smoking, caffeine, and exercise for at least `30` minutes beforehand
- take readings as your care team recommends

## Treatment

### Section title

Care options that can help

### Intro

Treatment usually combines regular monitoring, daily habits, and sometimes medication. The right plan depends on your numbers, overall risk, other health conditions, and what is realistic for your life.

Some people begin with lifestyle changes. Some need medication right away. Many people need both.

### Your care plan may include

- a blood pressure goal
- home monitoring
- follow-up visits
- eating and sodium changes
- physical activity
- limiting or avoiding alcohol
- tobacco support
- sleep or stress support
- medication
- checks for related conditions

### Medication basics

Medication is sometimes needed to protect the heart, brain, blood vessels, kidneys, and eyes. If medication is prescribed, take it as directed and ask what side effects to watch for.

Do not stop, skip, or change medication without talking with your health care professional. Ask a clinician or pharmacist before starting over-the-counter medicines or supplements, because some can raise blood pressure or interact with blood pressure medication.

## Living with it

### Section title

Manage day to day

### Intro

Living with high blood pressure is usually about repeatable care, not one dramatic change. The work happens in meals, movement, medication routines, sleep, stress, appointments, and home readings.

The goal is to choose a few steps you can actually keep doing, then adjust with your care team as your numbers change.

### Everyday steps that can help

- Eat more vegetables, fruits, whole grains, beans, lentils, nuts, seeds, fish, and other heart-healthy foods.
- Compare labels and choose lower-sodium options when you can.
- Ask whether potassium-rich foods are right for you, especially if you have kidney disease or take medicines that affect potassium.
- Move more in ways you can repeat. Many adults aim for at least `150` minutes of moderate activity each week, but smaller starts still count.
- Limit or avoid alcohol.
- Get support for stress, sleep, tobacco, medication routines, and appointment follow-up.

### Starter plan

Choose one step for this week:

- check your blood pressure at the same time for a few days
- replace one high-sodium food with a lower-sodium option
- take a `10` minute walk after a meal
- set a medication reminder
- write down questions for your next appointment

## Page-end module

### Still have questions?

High blood pressure care is personal. Your numbers, health history, medications, pregnancy status, age, and overall risk can change what you should do next. Use this guide to understand the topic, then talk with your health care professional about the plan that fits you.

### Useful next steps

- Bring recent readings to your next appointment.
- Ask what blood pressure goal is right for you.
- Ask whether home monitoring would help.
- Ask which daily change would matter most for your numbers.
- Ask before stopping medication or starting over-the-counter medicines or supplements.
