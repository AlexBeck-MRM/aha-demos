Medicines That Can Affect Blood Pressure

Some prescription medicines, over-the-counter medicines, supplements, and everyday substances can raise blood pressure or make it harder to control. This does not mean you should stop taking them. It means they are worth reviewing with a health care professional.

Reviewed by AHA health experts For patients and caregivers Part of Causes

Blood pressure is shaped by more than one thing at a time. Age, family history, sleep, stress, other health conditions, food, alcohol, tobacco, access to care, and medicines can all influence readings. That is why a medication review is often part of high blood pressure care.

For many people, medicine is protective and necessary. A medication for pain, mood, allergies, contraception, inflammation, a cold, or another condition may still be the right choice. The important point is to know whether it could be affecting blood pressure, whether there is a safer option, and whether your care team should monitor readings more closely while you use it.

Why a medication review matters

A medication review helps connect the blood pressure pattern with the full picture of what someone is taking. This includes prescriptions from different clinicians, medicines bought without a prescription, vitamins, supplements, pain relievers, cold and allergy products, stimulants, nicotine products, alcohol, and anything taken only occasionally.

The review is not about blame. It is a safety step. A person may have repeated high readings and assume the only answer is stronger blood pressure treatment, when another medicine or substance is part of the pattern. Or the opposite may be true: the medicine may not be the issue, and the review can help the care team look elsewhere.

Do not stop a medicine suddenly.

Some medicines can cause harm if they are stopped or changed without medical guidance. If you think a medicine is affecting blood pressure, write down the name, dose, when you take it, and what readings you are seeing. Then talk with a clinician or pharmacist.

What to bring to the conversation

Bring a complete list, not just the medicines you think are important. Include the dose, how often you take it, why you take it, and whether you sometimes skip it or take extra. If you use a medication only when symptoms flare, include that too. A cold medicine taken for a few days, a pain reliever used during a difficult week, or a supplement started recently can still be relevant.

It also helps to bring recent blood pressure readings. A single number from memory is less useful than a small pattern: morning and evening readings, the time they were taken, whether medicine had already been taken, and anything unusual that day such as poor sleep, pain, stress, alcohol, tobacco, caffeine, or heavy activity.

What may affect readings

Several categories can matter for some people. Pain relievers, decongestants, stimulants, some antidepressants, steroids, hormone-related medicines, some herbal products, nicotine, and alcohol can affect blood pressure in different ways. The details depend on the person, the dose, how often it is used, and what other health conditions are present.

This is why the page should not try to turn into a long medication database. The better user experience is to help people recognize that medicines can be part of the picture, then prepare them to ask a better question. The deeper clinical answer belongs with a health care professional who can see the medication list, health history, pregnancy status, kidney function, and overall risk.

Questions to ask

Use these as conversation starters rather than a checklist to solve alone.

  • Could any medicine, supplement, or over-the-counter product I use be affecting my blood pressure?
  • Is there an alternative that is safer for someone with high blood pressure?
  • Should I check my blood pressure more often while taking this medicine?
  • What side effects or readings should prompt me to call?

If you are helping someone else

Caregivers can make this easier by helping keep one current medication list, bringing bottles or photos of labels to appointments, and writing down questions before the visit. The person with high blood pressure should still be involved in decisions whenever possible. The goal is shared clarity, not taking over.

If there is confusion about what someone is actually taking, ask a pharmacist or care team member for a medication review. That can be especially useful after a hospital stay, a new diagnosis, a change in insurance coverage, or a visit with more than one clinician.
