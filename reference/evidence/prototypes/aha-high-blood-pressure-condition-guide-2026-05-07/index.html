01

Overview

Start with the basics.

Blood pressure is the force of blood pushing against the walls of your arteries. It changes during the day. Over time, high pressure can strain the heart, brain, blood vessels, kidneys, and eyes.

High blood pressure is also called hypertension. It is common, often silent, and manageable. Start with the numbers, the warning signs that need quick action, and the pattern created by repeated readings.

Use recent readings, symptoms, medicines, daily routines, and questions to prepare for a clearer conversation with your care team.

What the numbers mean

Measured readings are the clearest way to know whether blood pressure is high.

  • Systolic pressure is the top number.
  • Diastolic pressure is the bottom number.
  • The unit is mm Hg.
  • Your care team reviews patterns over time.

Key facts

  • High blood pressure often has no symptoms.
  • Your numbers matter when you feel well.
  • One high reading needs a recheck and a record.
  • Repeated high readings need review by a health care professional.
  • Medication, daily habit changes, or both may be part of care.
  • Very high readings with symptoms need emergency care.

Numbers at a glance

Category Top number Bottom number
Normal Less than 120 And less than 80
Elevated 120 to 129 And less than 80
Stage 1 high blood pressure 130 to 139 Or 80 to 89
Stage 2 high blood pressure 140 or higher Or 90 or higher
Very high Higher than 180 And/or higher than 120

Why it matters

Uncontrolled high blood pressure can damage blood vessels over time. It can raise the chance of stroke, heart attack, heart failure, kidney problems, vision loss, and other cardiovascular problems.

What people often miss

  • Feeling well can happen with high blood pressure.
  • Medication is a common part of care.
  • Blood pressure and heart rate are different measurements.

Common questions

Is high blood pressure the same as hypertension?

Yes. Hypertension is the medical term for high blood pressure.

Can I have high blood pressure and feel normal?

Yes. Many people with high blood pressure feel well. Regular checks give you information that symptoms may never provide.

Does one high reading mean I have high blood pressure?

A single reading can be affected by stress, pain, activity, caffeine, tobacco, timing, or cuff fit. Recheck it, record it, and ask a health care professional whether your readings show a pattern.

Why does this matter if I feel okay?

High blood pressure can strain blood vessels and organs over time, including during periods when you feel well.

02

Signs

Know what to check.

High blood pressure often develops without obvious symptoms. A measured reading is usually the first sign that pressure may be high.

Record the number, time of day, symptoms, medicines taken or missed, and anything that may have affected the reading. A clear record helps patients, caregivers, and clinicians see patterns.

Treat chest pain, shortness of breath, weakness, vision changes, or trouble speaking as serious symptoms. A very high reading with serious symptoms needs urgent action.

What to notice

Record the number and note how the person feels. Chest pain, shortness of breath, weakness, vision changes, or trouble speaking can make the situation urgent.

How to take a reading

  1. Use a validated upper-arm monitor when possible.
  2. Sit still with your back supported and feet flat.
  3. Keep your arm supported at heart level.
  4. Avoid smoking, exercise, or caffeine for at least 30 minutes beforehand.
  5. Write down the reading, time, and any symptoms or missed doses.

When a reading may be urgent

  1. If a reading is higher than 180/120 mm Hg, wait at least one minute and take it again.
  2. If it is still that high and you have serious symptoms, call 911.
  3. If it is still that high without serious symptoms, contact your health care professional right away.

Call 911 if a very high reading comes with

  • Chest pain
  • Shortness of breath
  • Back pain
  • Numbness or weakness
  • Vision changes
  • Difficulty speaking
  • Confusion or fainting

What to track

  • Recent readings
  • Time of day
  • Medicines taken or missed
  • New symptoms
  • Caffeine, alcohol, tobacco, stress, sleep, or activity changes
  • Questions for your care team

Common questions

Why do I feel fine if my reading is high?

High blood pressure often develops without obvious symptoms. A reading gives information that feelings alone cannot provide.

What number needs attention?

Ask your health care professional what goal is right for you. A reading higher than 180/120 mm Hg needs attention right away, especially when it stays high after a recheck or comes with serious symptoms.

Should I check my blood pressure at home?

Home monitoring is useful for many people. Ask your care team how often to check, what device to use, and what numbers should prompt a call.

What should I bring to an appointment?

Bring your reading log, medication list, questions, and notes about symptoms or missed doses.

How can I help someone else check their pressure?

Help them sit comfortably, use the cuff correctly, stay quiet during the reading, and write the result down. Leave diagnosis and medication decisions to a qualified health professional.

03

Causes

Review contributing factors.

High blood pressure is shaped by age, family history, health conditions, medicines, sleep, stress, access to care, and daily routines. Several factors may be involved at the same time.

Review your risk factors to make care more specific. Focus on what applies to you, what can be tracked, and what can be addressed with your care team.

When readings stay high, change suddenly, or remain above goal, bring a full picture to the visit: health history, medicines, routines, barriers, and recent readings.

Use causes as clinical clues

Notice what may apply, track what you can, and ask your care team what matters most for your readings.

Body and background factors

Some risk factors stay with you. They still help guide monitoring, treatment decisions, and the timing of follow-up.

  • Age: blood vessels can become stiffer over time, which can raise pressure.
  • Family history: high blood pressure can run in families.
  • Race and lived health inequities: access, stress, care quality, and social conditions can shape risk.
  • Pregnancy history: high blood pressure during pregnancy can matter for future heart and blood vessel risk.
  • Childhood or lifelong factors: early health conditions, weight changes, sleep patterns, and access to care can shape adult risk.

Daily patterns that can move readings

Daily routines can move readings. Choose one steady change you can repeat, then build from there.

  • Food pattern: fruits, vegetables, whole grains, beans, and lower-sodium foods can support blood pressure care.
  • Sodium: restaurant food, packaged food, sauces, breads, and deli meats can add up quickly.
  • Movement: regular activity can support blood pressure care, including small starts.
  • Alcohol and tobacco: both can affect blood pressure and overall heart risk.
  • Stress and sleep: stress can affect routines, hormones, sleep, medication timing, and short-term readings.
  • Medication routine: missed doses, side effects, cost, or unclear instructions can make readings harder to control.

Health conditions to ask about

Some conditions can raise blood pressure or make it harder to manage. If your readings stay high, ask whether any of these conditions should be reviewed.

  • Diabetes
  • Kidney disease
  • Sleep apnea
  • Thyroid or adrenal conditions
  • Blood pressure problems during pregnancy
  • Some heart and blood vessel conditions

Medicines and substances to review

Some prescription medicines, over-the-counter medicines, supplements, pain relievers, decongestants, stimulants, alcohol, and tobacco can affect blood pressure.

Keep taking prescribed medicine unless a clinician tells you to change it. Bring the full list to a clinician or pharmacist and ask what may be affecting your readings.

Make care fit real life

Cost, transportation, work schedules, food access, stress, caregiving responsibilities, language, trust, and appointment access can all affect care.

Tell your care team when a recommendation is hard to follow. Cost, access, side effects, and schedule problems are part of care.

What to bring up at a visit

Bring a clear picture of what has been happening when readings stay high or change suddenly. Your care team can look for patterns, contributors, and practical barriers.

Health history

  • Family history of high blood pressure, stroke, heart attack, kidney disease, or diabetes.
  • Blood pressure problems during pregnancy.
  • Kidney disease, diabetes, sleep apnea, thyroid conditions, or other diagnoses.

Medicines and routines

  • Prescription medicines, over-the-counter products, supplements, and pain relievers.
  • Missed doses, side effects, refill problems, or unclear instructions.
  • Alcohol, tobacco, caffeine, sleep, stress, and recent changes in activity or food.

Questions worth asking

  • Could another condition, medicine, or supplement be contributing?
  • Is my cuff size and measuring technique correct?
  • What reading should prompt a call?

Risk can come from health history, medicines, access, stress, sleep, pregnancy history, and daily routines.

Common questions

Did I cause my high blood pressure?

High blood pressure is usually shaped by many factors. Focus on the factors that can be reviewed, tracked, treated, or supported now.

Does family history matter?

Yes. Family history can raise risk. Healthy routines, monitoring, and medical care still matter.

Can stress cause high blood pressure?

Stress can affect habits, sleep, hormones, and short-term readings. Stress support may be one helpful part of care.

Can medicines raise blood pressure?

Some medicines and supplements can affect blood pressure. Ask a clinician or pharmacist if you are unsure.

Why can guidance differ by age, pregnancy history, or lived experience?

Risk, access to care, age, pregnancy history, and lived experience can change what care should consider.

04

Care

Know what care can include.

Care may include treatment, monitoring, follow-up, daily habits, and practical support to keep blood pressure in a safer range. Medication, home monitoring, food changes, movement, and regular conversations with your care team may all be part of the plan.

Work with your care team to set the right blood pressure goal, check progress, manage medicines, and address barriers such as cost, side effects, food access, work schedules, or stress.

Keep readings, refills, meals, transport, appointment questions, and follow-up instructions clear enough to act on between visits.

What care may include

Blood pressure care often brings several actions together. Your health care professional can help decide what applies to you.

  • A blood pressure goal
  • Home monitoring
  • Follow-up visits
  • Eating and sodium changes
  • Physical activity
  • Alcohol guidance
  • Tobacco support
  • Sleep and stress support
  • Medication
  • Condition checks

Medication basics

Medication may be needed to protect the heart, brain, blood vessels, kidneys, and eyes. Take prescribed medication as directed and ask what side effects to watch for.

Talk with your health care professional before stopping, skipping, or changing medication. Ask a clinician or pharmacist before starting over-the-counter medicines, supplements, pain relievers, or decongestants.

Daily habits that can support care

  • Eat more vegetables, fruits, whole grains, beans, lentils, nuts, seeds, fish, and other heart-healthy foods.
  • Compare labels and choose lower-sodium options when you can.
  • Ask whether potassium-rich foods are right for you.
  • Move more in ways you can keep doing.
  • Limit or avoid alcohol.
  • Get support for stress, sleep, tobacco, medication routines, and follow-up.

Home monitoring

Home readings can show patterns between visits. Keep regular care appointments and use medication guidance from your clinician.

  1. Use a validated device.
  2. Check at a consistent time if your care team recommends it.
  3. Take readings as instructed.
  4. Record the date, time, and numbers.
  5. Note symptoms, missed doses, or unusual stress.
  6. Share the pattern and the highest readings.

Follow-up and adjustment

Blood pressure care often changes over time. Readings, side effects, other conditions, pregnancy, age, access to food, cost, and stress can all change what support is needed.

  • Ask what range your care team wants you to aim for.
  • Ask when to call about a reading.
  • Bring medication, supplement, and side-effect questions.
  • Share barriers early, especially cost, access, transport, or work schedules.

If you are helping someone else

  • Help organize readings, medicines, and appointment notes.
  • Ask what number should prompt a call.
  • Learn the emergency signs.
  • Help with one realistic routine at a time.
  • Make tracking, refills, appointments, and meals easier without taking over.

If care is hard to keep up with

Care has to fit real life. Bring up cost, side effects, transportation, food access, stress, work hours, and caregiving demands early.

  • Ask whether there is a lower-cost medicine option.
  • Ask what side effects should prompt a call.
  • Ask which daily factor matters most for your readings right now.
  • Ask what support is available for cost, transport, food access, language, or follow-up.

If readings stay high

Persistently high readings may require medication adjustment, checks for other causes, or specialist guidance.

  1. Share the full reading pattern with your care team.
  2. Check whether the cuff size and technique are correct.
  3. Ask whether other medicines or conditions may be contributing.
  4. Ask whether resistant hypertension guidance applies.

Common questions

Can lifestyle changes really lower blood pressure?

Daily habits help many people lower readings. Medication may also be needed. The right approach depends on your numbers, risk, health history, and what you can realistically maintain.

Will I need medication forever?

Medication needs vary. Talk with your health care professional before stopping or changing medication, and bring your readings, side effects, and goals to the conversation.

What if I miss a dose?

Follow the instructions from your care team or pharmacist. Ask what to do after a missed dose. Take an extra dose only if a clinician tells you to.

What food change should I start with?

Start by noticing sodium. Compare labels, choose lower-sodium options when you can, and add heart-healthy foods you already like.

How much activity do I need?

Many adults aim for regular moderate activity. Small starts still count. Ask what is safe for you if you have symptoms, other conditions, or have been inactive.

What if my blood pressure stays high even with care?

Tell your health care professional. Some people need medication changes, checks for other causes, or specialist guidance.

How can a caregiver help without taking over?

Help with logs, reminders, appointments, meals, and questions. Keep the person involved in decisions whenever possible.
