### Tooltip Terms

Use inline tooltip links for plain-language explanations. Terms currently used include:

- hypertension: Another name for high blood pressure.
- arteries: Blood vessels that carry blood from the heart to the rest of the body.
- blood vessels: The network of tubes that carries blood through the body.
- systolic pressure: The top number in a blood pressure reading.
- diastolic pressure: The bottom number in a blood pressure reading.
- mm Hg: Millimeters of mercury.
- care team: Clinicians, pharmacists, family members, and support people helping with care.
- stroke: A medical emergency involving blocked or burst blood vessels in the brain.
- heart attack: A medical emergency involving blocked blood flow to part of the heart.
- heart failure: A condition where the heart cannot pump blood as well as the body needs.
- cardiovascular: Involving the heart and blood vessels.
- risk factor: Something that can raise the chance of a condition or health event.
- diabetes: A condition where blood sugar is higher than normal.
- kidney disease: Kidney problems that can affect fluid balance, minerals, and blood pressure.
- sleep apnea: A sleep disorder where breathing repeatedly stops or becomes shallow.
- thyroid or adrenal conditions: Hormone-linked conditions that can affect blood pressure.
- over-the-counter medicines: Medicines available without a prescription.
- supplements: Vitamins, minerals, herbs, or other products taken in addition to food or prescribed medicine.
- decongestants: Medicines that may raise blood pressure in some people.
- stimulants: Substances that can affect heart rate or blood pressure.
- home monitoring: Checking and recording blood pressure outside a clinic.
- blood pressure goal: A measurable aim set with a health care professional.
- sodium: A mineral in salt that can raise blood pressure for many people.
- potassium: A mineral that helps some people balance sodium. People with kidney disease or certain medicines need clinician guidance before increasing potassium.
- resistant hypertension: High blood pressure that stays above goal even when several medicines are used as directed.
- pulmonary hypertension: High blood pressure in the blood vessels between the heart and lungs.

### Emergency Cue

If your blood pressure is higher than 180/120 mm Hg, wait at least one minute and take it again. Call 911 right away if the second reading stays that high and you have chest pain, shortness of breath, back pain, numbness, weakness, vision changes, difficulty speaking, or another new concerning symptom.

## Section 1: Overview

### Section Intro

Blood pressure is the force of blood pushing against the walls of your arteries. It changes during the day. Over time, high pressure can strain the heart, brain, blood vessels, kidneys, and eyes.

High blood pressure is also called hypertension. It is common, often silent, and manageable. Start with the numbers, the warning signs that need quick action, and the pattern created by repeated readings.

Use recent readings, symptoms, medicines, daily routines, and questions to prepare for a clearer conversation with your care team.

## Section 2: Signs

### Section Intro

High blood pressure often develops without obvious symptoms. A measured reading is usually the first sign that pressure may be high.

Record the number, time of day, symptoms, medicines taken or missed, and anything that may have affected the reading. A clear record helps patients, caregivers, and clinicians see patterns.

Treat chest pain, shortness of breath, weakness, vision changes, or trouble speaking as serious symptoms. A very high reading with serious symptoms needs urgent action.

## Section 3: Causes

### Section Intro

High blood pressure is shaped by age, family history, health conditions, medicines, sleep, stress, access to care, and daily routines. Several factors may be involved at the same time.

Review your risk factors to make care more specific. Focus on what applies to you, what can be tracked, and what can be addressed with your care team.

When readings stay high, change suddenly, or remain above goal, bring a full picture to the visit: health history, medicines, routines, barriers, and recent readings.

## Section 4: Care

### Section Intro

Care may include treatment, monitoring, follow-up, daily habits, and practical support to keep blood pressure in a safer range. Medication, home monitoring, food changes, movement, and regular conversations with your care team may all be part of the plan.

Work with your care team to set the right blood pressure goal, check progress, manage medicines, and address barriers such as cost, side effects, food access, work schedules, or stress.

Keep readings, refills, meals, transport, appointment questions, and follow-up instructions clear enough to act on between visits.

## Visual And Interaction Rules

- Never use three-column or four-column content grids.
- Use a maximum of two columns at desktop.
- Detail-extension groups should stack vertically so each topic reads as a natural next step.
- Link cards inside detail-extension groups should be vertical lists.
- Where a stacked group leaves unused space, use a relevant greyscale illustration to support the narrative.
- On mobile, content stacks to one column.
- Use plain editorial text on the page background for core explanations.
- Use white cards only when the content has a module function: tools, tables, urgent callouts, questions, media, or grouped detail links.
- Avoid black divider lines inside content areas.
- Use spacing, soft card edges, and very light surface contrast.
- The section sidebar tracks the current scroll position and sets `aria-current` on the active section.
- Mobile section navigation scrolls the active item into view.
- Prototype subpage cards use placeholders until additional article pages are built; medication-related links route to the tested article detail page.
- Keep the final prototype folder clean: no preview screenshots, Figma transfer captures, redirect stubs, or unused icon placeholders.
