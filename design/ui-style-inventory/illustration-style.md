# Illustration Style

## Direction
Make the Route 2 illustration language the one primary AHA illustration system, but clean up the colour balance so it no longer reads too warm, brown, or beige. Keep the no-outline flat-fill construction, adult proportions, simple scenes, and one clear AHA-red action signal. Shift the base palette toward white, off-white, cool bone, pale stone grey, taupe-grey, charcoal, AHA red, and deep red. Use warmer browns only where they have a reason: skin, hair, food, wood, or very small shadow moments. Add light pastel tints only when the subject benefits from them, especially nature, water, sleep, calm, or produce. The system should feel useful and human on AHA pages, but still clean enough to sit inside dense navigation, guide cards, education modules, and condition-page tools.

## Reasoning
- The reference board gives us a clearer primary system than the earlier contour-led route: no-outline construction, disciplined neutrals, simple adult figures, and red used as the action or emotional anchor.
- The attached palette reference points to a better balance: white and pale monochrome neutrals first, red as the brand signal, and taupe/charcoal as definition rather than atmosphere.
- This better supports the canonical need for a white-first, trustworthy ecosystem because the illustrations can add humanity without adding visual noise or a muddy wellness cast.
- The system can work harder across UX surfaces: tiny icons, navigation cues, guide cards, education modules, tool prompts, and larger editorial headers can all share the same palette, shape logic, and red-signal behavior.
- AHA red should stay purposeful. It should mark the thing to notice, do, choose, or feel, not become a background wash or repeated decoration.
- Secondary pastels help topic clarity when they are tied to subject matter, but they should stay quiet enough that they do not compete with red or turn into a separate colourful illustration route.
- More complex scenes are allowed, but they need to stay magazine-like through composition, cropping, and subject specificity rather than through extra detail, texture, or scenic realism.
- The main risk is generic nonprofit wellness illustration. We should avoid interchangeable metaphors by anchoring scenes in AHA-relevant symbols and actions: food choices, movement, sleep routines, stress support, smoking/vaping cessation, weight-management tools, CPR, blood pressure, care conversations, and practical habit tracking.

## Locked properties
- One primary system, four density levels. The dialect should not change between icons, spots, instructional scenes, and editorial headers.
- Use no-outline construction. Build figures, props, furniture, backplates, and ground planes from adjacent flat fills, overlaps, negative space, and value contrast.
- Palette stays narrow and quiet: white, off-white, cool bone, light stone grey, taupe-grey, charcoal, AHA red, and deep red form the core. Warm stone and brown are demoted to purposeful detail. Secondary pastels are allowed only for subject-specific cues.
- Figures are simplified but adult and grounded: stable torsos, believable hands, calm faces, and no oversized heads or mascot proportions. Identity should come from silhouette, hair shape, glasses, gesture, and context before facial detail.
- Faces are minimal: simple profile or front-facing shape, optional eye/nose marks only when needed, no expressive portrait detail.
- Shading stays sparse and broad. Use broad tonal blocks and quiet shadows only. No modelled faces, rendered hands, hair shine, cloth-fold shading, plaid or pattern rendering, or glossy gradients.
- Shapes stay broad and economical. Use fewer, better-placed forms instead of lots of tiny prop detail or decorative fragments.
- Perspective stays shallow and editorial. Depth should come from layered flats, crop, isometric hints, and one quiet support field rather than scenic realism or big atmospheric blobs.
- Red appears once or twice per scene as a focal signal: shirt, heart, checklist tick, blood pressure reading, CPR/AED object, data point, walking placard, meal bowl, or selected UI cue.

## Colour palette
- White `#ffffff`: page canvas, board background, and open negative space.
- Off white `#f4f3f3`: lightest support surfaces.
- Soft white `#f5f4f2`: warm-white planes where pure white needs separation.
- Cool bone `#eeece9`: default neutral fill for large soft shapes.
- Light stone grey `#e1e0dc`: secondary planes, support shapes, and quiet depth.
- Warm stone `#ddd8d2`: limited warmth only; do not let this become the dominant scene colour.
- Taupe grey `#7d7971`: muted clothing, hair, props, and secondary definition.
- Charcoal `#3d3c3a`: hair, device details, small functional marks, and the crispest definition points.
- Black `#000000`: rare micro-detail only.
- AHA red `#e6001b`: the primary action, emotion, selection, or brand signal.
- Deep red `#bf0000`: depth inside red objects only.

## Secondary pastels
- Water / air tint `#edf2f3`: water, sky, breathing, cool clinical support, and soft environmental backplates.
- Nature tint `#e7f0eb`: leaves, plants, outdoor paths, habit cues, and calm wellbeing moments.
- Soft red tint `#f6e8e9`: low-pressure support around red objects or selected states.
- Pale oat `#f5ebdd`: food, grains, wood, or seasonal detail only; keep it rare because it can pull the style back toward beige.

## Colour ratio
- Use `70-80%` white, off-white, cool bone, and pale grey stone.
- Use `10-15%` taupe-grey, charcoal, skin, hair, and object definition.
- Use `5-10%` AHA red and deep red.
- Use `0-5%` secondary pastel tints, only when tied to the subject.
- Do not let brown, taupe, warm stone, oat, or skin tones become the first thing someone notices.

## Zone scaling
- Zone 1 icons: diagrammatic, one to three flat shapes, no environment, red as the recognition cue.
- Zone 2 card spots: one subject or one interaction, one support shape, minimal props, strong silhouette readability at small sizes.
- Zone 3 instructional scenes: two to three figures, a clear action, one prop cluster, and just enough setting to explain the task.
- Zone 4 editorial scenes: wider crop, stronger rhythm, and more layered composition. The scene can feel magazine-like, but it should still use flat fills, controlled palette, simple faces, and no-outline construction.
- Topic grids: do not force people into every tile. Use symbolic still-life compositions and icon-derived objects first, then add hands or people only if the topic needs human action.

## Fixed vs variable motifs
- Fixed: no-outline construction, adult proportions, clean monochrome-stone base, purposeful red signal, minimal shading, calm expressions, simple props, and scene-first readability.
- Variable: activity, crop, number of people, prop density, support shape, isometric plane, and how editorial the composition becomes.

## Failure modes to avoid
- Too much colour or accent drift outside the locked palette.
- Brown, tan, warm stone, or taupe becoming the dominant read of a scene.
- Saturated clothing blocks that overpower the scene instead of one controlled red cue.
- Portrait-style crops that prioritize likeness over scene clarity.
- Too much facial, hair, hand, or fabric detail.
- Heavy shading, glossy gradients, or rendered volume.
- Any stroked-outline logic around people, props, furniture, backplates, or ground planes.
- Generic wellness metaphors that could belong to any health-tech or nonprofit brand.
- Cute, childish, mascot-like, or overly rounded figures.
- Locally fabricated SVG, PIL, or code-drawn substitutes when an actual raster image generation is requested. If image generation is blocked, provide a prompt instead of making a fake image fallback.

## Primary reference: Route 2
Use the Route 2 reference board as the primary style anchor: small flat vector vignettes, lots of white space, simple people, clear props, pale support shapes, and one direct AHA-red action signal. The move is not to keep the current site's stock-like softness as-is. It is to make that familiar, lightweight language sharper, cleaner, more ownable, and more useful inside dense journeys where illustration needs to support wayfinding, education, and action.

### Style description
- Overall mode: flat editorial vector, closer to compact web illustration than narrative poster art.
- Composition: isolated vignettes on white or off-white fields, often with one person or a small group, one prop cluster, and a quiet support shape.
- Figure language: simplified adult bodies, small heads, calm posture, minimal faces, simplified hands, and identity carried by hair, clothing, gesture, and context.
- Shape language: broad fills, rounded organic backplates, simple rectangular props, occasional isometric planes, and very little texture.
- Linework: no outlines. Build figures and props from adjacent flat fills, overlaps, negative space, and small shape details. Use tiny interior marks only when functionally necessary, such as a checklist tick, graph point, device reading, or facial dot.
- Colour: keep to the locked palette from `color-scheme.md`: white `#ffffff`, off-white `#f4f3f3`, soft white `#f5f4f2`, cool bone `#eeece9`, light stone grey `#e1e0dc`, taupe grey `#7d7971`, charcoal `#3d3c3a`, AHA red `#e6001b`, and deep red `#bf0000`. Use warm stone `#ddd8d2` and secondary pastels only when the subject needs them.
- Red behavior: use red as the action or emotional anchor: cape, heart, shirt, checklist mark, CPR pad, donation object, or data point. Avoid red wash backgrounds.
- Depth: shallow and diagrammatic. Use one broad shadow, one support blob, or one simple plane; avoid atmospheric scenes, detailed rooms, and rendered lighting.
- Metaphor: allowed, but keep it literal enough to support comprehension. The cape, heart, checklist, and community table work because they read instantly.
- Finish: clean, friendly, accessible, and slightly instructional. It should feel useful before it feels decorative.

### No-outline rules
- Do not stroke figure silhouettes, clothing edges, hair shapes, props, furniture, backplates, or ground planes.
- Separate forms through value contrast, overlap, gaps, and shape silhouette.
- Keep interior detail sparse and functional: check marks, simple graph lines, small UI marks, minimal eyes, and essential device indicators only.
- If a scene needs contour lines to be legible, simplify the pose or prop instead of adding outlines.
- Avoid warm contour behavior; that belongs to the previous illustration route, not the primary Route 2 system.

### Editorial expansion rules
- Let larger scenes feel more magazine-like through asymmetry, crop, layered people, and a clearer point of view.
- Use more specific AHA moments instead of generic wellness scenes: hands-only CPR practice, grocery choice, walking group, bedtime routine, care-team conversation, smoke-free milestone, or blood pressure tracking.
- Keep environments reduced to a few useful anchors: table, counter, bed, walking path, clinic chair, device, chart, plant, window, or simple skyline.
- Do not add texture, pattern, scenery, or facial expression to create drama. The drama should come from composition, gesture, red signal, and the editorial crop.
- Any larger illustration should be reducible into a card spot or icon family by removing setting, then props, then secondary figures.

### Use cases to test
- Program and action tiles: Heart Walk, Go Red, fundraising, advocacy, volunteer moments.
- Health-management tools: blood pressure tracking, heart failure self-checks, medication routines, symptom escalation.
- Learning modules: CPR steps, warning signs, appointment prep, nutrition swaps.
- Navigation support: mega-menu panels, related-content cards, journey entry points, lightweight empty states.
- Healthy Living guides: healthy eating, quitting smoking and vaping, exercise and fitness, mental wellbeing, sleep, and weight management.

### Healthy Living topic symbols
- Healthy Eating: apple, bowl, balanced plate, nutrition label, grocery/meal-planning cue.
- Exercise and Fitness: shoe, path, step/motion cue, simple activity signal.
- Sleep: crescent moon, bed pillow, alarm toggle, quiet night support shape.
- Mental Wellbeing: calm/breathing mark, simple meditation cue, stacked stones, heart-in-circle, journal/check cue.
- Quit Smoking and Vaping: no-smoking/no-vaping symbol, crossed-out vape or cigarette, quit-plan card, red check.
- Weight Management: scale, progress path, balanced plate, habit tracker, supportive check cue.

### Example moments
- A volunteer teaches hands-only CPR with a red AED/heart signal and a simple off-white floor plane.
- A person checks blood pressure at a kitchen table with a red reading marker and a small checklist.
- A Heart Walk group gathers around a simple red banner or heart placard, with bodies reduced to clear silhouettes.
- A family prepares a heart-healthy meal with one red ingredient bowl, pale grey-bone counter shapes, and a small nature or food tint only if it clarifies the scene.
- A person swaps a vape for a red check-mark card and a simple support-message phone cue.
- A person winds down beside a bed with a red alarm toggle, simplified moon/window shape, and quiet pale grey-blue support field.
