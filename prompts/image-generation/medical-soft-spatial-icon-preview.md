# AHA Medical Soft Spatial Icon Preview Prompt

## Use
Use this prompt to generate a focused raster reference sheet for the refined middle spatial icon direction. This evolves the prior Lava Object route into a cleaner medical-spatial style: flatter, calmer, less textured, less cute, more real-object based, and still gently dimensional.

This is a style preview, not production icon export.

## Direction
Use one icon language only: `Medical Soft Spatial`.

The icons should feel like calm, representative 3D medical objects designed for a serious health website. Keep the soft spatial feel, but remove the excess texture, clay-like modelling, heavy shadow, block-kit figure construction, and soft toy-like character from the previous preview. The result should be clean, restrained, useful, realistic enough to feel medical, and warm without becoming cute.

## Reference analysis
The useful part of the attached reference style is not full realism. It is the way familiar objects become small, legible, spatial miniatures on white:

- The viewpoint is consistent: a gentle three-quarter view with a little top surface visible, not dramatic perspective.
- The icons are object-led. Most subjects are explained through a single recognizable prop or compact object cluster, not through characters.
- The realism comes from proportion and material logic: a bottle looks like a bottle, fabric reads as fabric, a map folds like paper, and a bag has believable volume.
- Colour is natural and semantic. Green means produce or landscape, tan means paper or wood, blue means water or fabric, red means a signal or focal object.
- Shadows are soft and close. They ground the objects without turning them into full product renders.
- The white background and isolated square spacing make dense subjects feel usable.

The parts to avoid for AHA are the higher detail level and lifestyle-object warmth: kernels, noodles, wood grain, tiny map markings, many little contents in a bag, stronger browns, and food-like texture. Those details help the reference feel charming, but they would make AHA icons too busy and too cute at small UI sizes.

## AHA adaptation
Apply the reference as `credible miniature realism`, not as a literal style copy:

- Keep AHA first: neutral medical greys, clean white surfaces, restrained red signal, calm clinical usefulness.
- Borrow the believable object proportions, consistent camera angle, and soft spatial grounding.
- Use natural colour only when it speeds up recognition: produce green/red, tobacco tan, clinical blue grey, muted path green, controlled red liquid, or care/support red.
- Strip each subject down harder than the reference. One main object, one signal, and at most one support cue.
- Avoid the reference's lifestyle warmth when it conflicts with AHA: no wood-heavy props, rustic browns, cute food texture, decorative clutter, or travel/lifestyle icon energy.
- Keep people abstract and rare. Prefer objects, hands, torsos, or action cues over small character scenes.

## Realism dial
Use a middle level of realism:

- Too flat: loses the new spatial benefit.
- Too cute: puffy toy forms, oversized heads, emoji hearts, plush objects.
- Too realistic: photographic skin, hair, food texture, labels, tiny markings, heavy material detail, product-render lighting.
- Target: simplified real-world objects with believable proportions, broad matte surfaces, restrained natural colour, and small-size clarity.

## Design rules
- Keep the style flatter than a product render, but more spatial than flat vector icons.
- Increase realism through believable object proportions, cleaner construction, and subject-specific material cues. Realism means credible forms, not photographic detail.
- Stay in the icon-illustration register. Do not render photographic skin, hair, faces, food texture, fabric weave, tiny gauge numbers, tiny calendar marks, or realistic product labels.
- Think simplified medical product photography translated into broad 3D icons, not clay characters, toy objects, or literal photos.
- Use a consistent gentle three-quarter view, with a small amount of top plane visible. Avoid front-flat vector staging and avoid dramatic isometric scenes.
- Use smooth matte surfaces with very low texture. No grain, clay speckle, roughness, mottling, or tactile noise.
- Use shallow side planes, small overlaps, clean cut-ins, and soft ambient contact shadows to create depth.
- Use soft diffuse studio lighting, closer to an overcast lightbox than a directional key light.
- Keep shadows short, soft, pale, and close to the object. Shadows should feel like a gentle grounding haze, not dark cast shadows or heavy ambient occlusion.
- Lift dark values across the set. Dark greys should be used only for small functional accents, never broad interiors, large side planes, or heavy object bases.
- Avoid dramatic cast shadows, floating objects, hard key-light direction, strong contact darkness, dark object interiors, and heavy ground darkness.
- Keep forms broad and simple. Each icon should read on white website cards, nav surfaces, and small content modules.
- Keep the medical feel clean and hygienic: precise silhouettes, controlled geometry, clear object metaphors, and no decorative filler.
- Keep contrast moderate. Use value separation to clarify forms, but avoid black, harsh outlines, deep dark interiors, or high-contrast product-render drama.
- Keep the icons mature. Use fewer puffy forms, fewer oversized rounded heads, and fewer friendly toy proportions. Avoid childish proportions, toy construction, plush shapes, sticker energy, emoji hearts, cute faces, and mascot-like figures.
- People should be simplified but more natural than block figures. Use neutral clinical clothing, cropped hands, torsos, or simple profile cues where possible. Keep heads smaller and more proportional. Use softer anatomical hints, natural posture, gently tapered limbs, and clear action poses. No square peg bodies, cube limbs, faceless mannequins with toy proportions, large circular heads, or doll-like bodies.
- Keep human forms secondary to the action. CPR and volunteer/community can include simplified people only if needed, but care and appointment icons should be object-led. Prefer objects, hands, torsos, and action cues over small character scenes.
- Avoid photorealistic people. No detailed faces, eyes, hair, skin tones, skin pores, fingernails, individual fingers, or identity-specific human features. Use neutral simplified silhouettes, simplified mitten-like hands, cropped torsos, and clinical clothing blocks.
- Keep every icon grounded in light website greys first. Use colour only where it transfers information.
- Use red as a signal for urgency, pulse, AED, alert, selection, or heart-specific meaning. Red should be cleanly separated from darker neutrals by white, pale grey, or negative space.
- Do not use text-like markings: no letters, numerals, currency symbols, dollar signs, labels, or product marks. Use abstract graphic signals instead.
- Do not bury red inside brown, taupe, clay, or muddy neutral areas.
- Avoid large floating red hearts. If a heart is needed, make it small, integrated, and functional rather than emotional shorthand.
- Natural colour can return when it clarifies subject matter, but only as one broad support cue per icon. Use natural colour as broad simple masses for food, plants/path, medicine, breath/sleep, smoke/tobacco, and medical glass/liquid when it improves recognition. Do not render detailed produce texture, skin colour, hair colour, labels, or photographic material detail.
- Sleep needs stronger contrast than a single pale-blue object. Use a pale neutral base, a medium neutral main form, and a small cool support tint.
- Avoid dense props. Use one main subject, one information or action signal, and zero or one support shape.
- Use a strict detail budget: no more than two or three main masses per icon, no more than one small signal detail, and no detail that disappears at 24-32 px.
- Design for small-size recognition: no tiny internal details, no thin decorative lines, no miniature scenes, no small numerals, no labels, no detailed dials, no visible facial features, and no reliance on subtle character detail.

## Prompt

All square containers must be equal size with even spacing and generous gutters. Do not draw containers, borders, labels, headings, numbers, logos, UI chrome, watermark, or any visible text.
